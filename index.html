Abstract

Audio textures exhibit complex temporal dynamics that are not well represented by single-scale models. We propose a multi-scale latent trajectory approach for audio texture synthesis using representations from a pre-trained neural audio codec. By decomposing latent trajectories into slow structural and fast stochastic components, we enable direct modeling and interpretation of the long-term temporal structure and fine grained detail of audio textures.

Experiments on environmental audio textures suggest that the proposed method significantly improves audio quality over a single-scale baseline and achieves performance competitive with a latent diffusion model. Using perceptually motivated summary statistics, we demonstrate that generated continuations preserve texture identity.

Through the proposed methods we enable continuous texture generation from a given context and expose a latent structure that may support control over long-term dynamics.

Proposed Method

Our proposed method aims to enable perceptually coherent and controllable audio texture synthesis. We achieve this by modeling a neural audio codec's latent trajectories at multiple temporal scales. The proposed multi-scale latent trajectory framework consists of a slow-rate variational autoencoder that captures long-term structural evolution and learns a compact latent representation for interactive manipulation, and a fast-rate conditional autoregressive model that generates local stochastic detail. Together, these components enable structured yet diverse texture generation.

Latent Trajectory Decomposition

We decompose latent audio trajectories into slow and fast components using Gaussian low-pass filtering, where the slow signal captures long-term structure and the fast signal represents local residual variation. By sweeping filter sizes, we evaluate how well this separation works using frequency and temporal metrics.

Figure: Shows that with appropriate filtering, the decomposition cleanly separates structured, slowly evolving dynamics from short-term, noise-like fluctuations, with each component exhibiting distinct statistical properties.

Modeling Overview

We model the decomposed latent trajectory using a two-scale approach, where the slow component (long-term structure) is generated with a VAE from context, and the fast component (local variation) is produced by an autoregressive model conditioned on the slow signal. During synthesis, the two are combined to form the final trajectory, enabling coherent long-term evolution alongside diverse, stochastic detail.

Slow-Rate Model

The slow-rate model uses a VAE to generate long-term trajectory structure from a context segment. A GRU-based encoder maps the context to a latent vector, which conditions a decoder to produce the continuation. The model can iteratively extend textures by feeding generated trajectories back in as new context, enabling coherent long-duration synthesis while maintaining controllable global structure.

Fast-Rate Model

The fast-rate model generates the high-frequency residual using an autoregressive RNN conditioned on the slow trajectory. At each step, it predicts a stochastic residual sample based on the previous output and current slow trajectory, capturing local, stochastic dynamics. This sequential process enables flexible, long-duration generation of fine-grained detail.

Results

Audio Quality

The results show that our proposed methods, the multi-scale approach significantly improves audio quality over the single-scale baseline, achieving much lower FAD scores and demonstrating the benefit of separating slow structure from fast variation. When trained per category, performance further improves and becomes competitive with latent diffusion, despite a simpler modeling approach.

Texture Identity

The results show that our proposed method, the multi-scale model preserves texture identity, producing summary statistics consistently close to real continuations across all measures. It outperforms the single-scale baseline and shows more stable behavior than latent diffusion, particularly for higher-order and modulation features, highlighting the benefit of explicitly modeling slow and fast dynamics separately.

While objective metrics and qualitative listening provide insight into model performance, a comprehensive evaluation of perceptual quality requires controlled listening studies. We plan to conduct formal listening experiments in future work to assess perceptual realism, continuity, and user interaction with the latent space.

Static Interpolations

The proposed model is not explicitly trained for controllability. However, by separating slow and fast dynamics, it exposes a structured latent representation of long-term texture evolution. This latent space can be probed and manipulated, providing a potential interface for future control-oriented systems.

These interpolations provide evidence that the latent space encodes smooth and meaningful variations in texture structure. While not a full control interface, this suggests that the representation could support interactive manipulation of long-term dynamics.
